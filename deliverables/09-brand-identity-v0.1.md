## If you only read one section

We are the person a founder calls the day they realize the front-desk chaos is unsustainable. Not a software company. Not a big consultancy. Not an agency. A small, senior team that reads your business, builds specific automation on tools you already own, and hands you back the hours you lost to admin. ## 1. Positioning statement

**For** founder-run small businesses (veterinary clinics, insurance agencies, DTC brands doing $500K–$5M) **who** are losing 15+ hours a week to operational admin that scaled with the business but can't be delegated to existing staff, **Tack** provides **AI-augmented operations consulting** that **audits their workflow, implements specific automations on tools they already own (Make/Zapier/n8n), and hands the hours back.** Unlike **enterprise consultancies** (too expensive, too slow, too abstract), **software vendors** (sell a tool, leave the founder to figure it out), or **fractional COOs** (expensive full-time hire, not scoped to a project), we **deliver a fixed-fee 5-week Implementation with a defined scope, a written liability cap, and a working system at the end.**

## 2. Personality — 5 adjectives, 5 anti-adjectives

**We are:**

1. **Grounded** — we describe what actually happens ("we'll audit for a week, then build for four") rather than what sounds impressive ("transformation," "synergy," "AI-first"). No fog.
2. **Precise** — pricing is fixed. Scope is written. Deliverables are named. If we say Thursday, we mean Thursday.
3. **Warm** — we like our clients. We take a call at 4:47 PM without an eyeroll. We remember what the vet's dog is named. This is not a personality trait for marketing — it's the actual difference between us and BigCo.
4. **Modern** — we use current tools well. We don't hide the fact that Claude does substantive analysis. We don't hide that a human reviews everything. Both are the deal.
5. **Direct** — we say what we think. If a client's asking for the wrong thing, we tell them and offer an alternative. If we can't help, we tell them who can.

**We are NOT:**

1. **Not startup-coded.** No cutesy mascots, no "we're just a scrappy team of dreamers," no gradient rainbow logos, no exclamation points in headlines. Our buyers own real businesses and want real vendors.
2. **Not enterprise-coded.** No stock photos of diverse people pointing at whiteboards, no "leveraging synergies to unlock digital transformation," no six-page proposals, no logos of Fortune 500s we've never worked with.
3. **Not AI-boosterism-coded.** No robot iconography, no "the future of work is here" copy, no dashboards full of gauges nobody reads. The AI is a tool, not the story.
4. **Not agency-coded.** No portfolio pages showing 47 recent projects, no dark-mode-forever aesthetic, no "we're a full-service creative studio." We're a consultancy, not a design shop.
5. **Not chatty.** No blog full of listicles, no "5 automation trends for 2026," no newsletter with a wave emoji greeting. We publish rarely and mean it when we do.

## 3. Voice — how we sound in writing

**Three principles:**

**a. Say the thing.** No warm-up sentences. Every paragraph earns its position. "Your front desk is drowning" is a better opener than "In today's fast-paced world of veterinary care...".

**b. Use specifics.** "You're spending 12 hours a week on insurance authorizations" beats "operational overhead is a challenge." Specifics build credibility; abstractions burn it.

**c. Own the trade-off.** Every recommendation has a downside. Say it out loud. "This will save you 8 hours a week, but the first two weeks will feel harder while your team adjusts to the new workflow" is a more trustworthy sentence than the same claim without the caveat.

**Voice tests (samples we should be able to write in this voice tomorrow):**

- Homepage headline: "Get your Wednesday afternoons back." — not "AI-Powered Operations Consulting for the Modern SMB."
- Pricing page one-liner: "Fixed fees, written scope, working system at handoff." — not "Transparent pricing designed to align with your business goals."
- Discovery-call CTA: "Book a 30-minute call. We'll ask what's eating your week." — not "Schedule a free consultation to explore how our solutions can transform your business."
- Bad-news email opener: "The vendor lock-in on your PMS is worse than we hoped." — not "We've encountered some challenges with the integration we wanted to explore with you."

**Anti-reference set (things we should look and sound nothing like — direct input from Robert 2026-06-27):**

1. **[Zaka.ai](https://zaka.ai)** — dark navy + electric cyan gradient AI aesthetic. "Your Artificial Intelligence Transformation" headline. Geometric layered visuals. This is the entire "post-2022 AI consulting startup" playbook. We are not this.

**What the pattern tells us about direction:**

- All three aspirational refs are **navy-primary, sans-serif throughout, real-photography-heavy**. Not warm-craft (cream + serif) — heritage-professional (navy + sans + real photos).
- All three land as **premium without being corporate-generic**. That's the tightrope: rich color and typography discipline (premium) but distinctive real photography and specific copy (not generic).
- Cultivate's "Partner with an expert" is the closest voice match. Orases's trust-cue architecture (NPS, client logos, U.S.-based) is a template we should adapt once we have social proof. Land Rover's imperative headline treatment is the confidence tone we should aim for.
- Robert's dislikes cluster on two failure modes: **AI-startup gradient aesthetic (Zaka)** and **neglected generic corporate (Yoh, Columbus)**. Both make our website look like we don't take our own business seriously.

## 5. Color direction — v0.1.1 palette proposal (revised)

**Primary color: deep navy.**

All three aspirational reference brands (Orases, Cultivate, Land Rover) use deep navy as their primary. This is not a coincidence — navy signals trust, permanence, and premium-professional. It's the color our buyers already associate with "serious grown-up firm." Green would read craft/warm; navy reads authority.

**My call: deep navy.** Specifically a slightly warmer navy than pure Prussian — closer to Land Rover's iconic "British Racing Blue"-adjacent shade than to Silicon Valley Y-Combinator navy. **#0F2540** as v0.1.1 anchor. Adjust in real design pass.

**Background: crisp white or near-white.**

Not cream. Cream reads warm-craft (Squarespace, Mailchimp); we want heritage-premium. All three aspirational refs use near-white. Use **#FFFFFF** for hero and primary sections, **#F7F8F9** (near-white gray) for alternating section backgrounds.

**Accent color: warm teal OR burnt orange.**

Cultivate uses teal + orange as a dual-accent system — teal for interactive UI (links, hover states), orange for CTA buttons and highlights. This is the pattern to steal. Teal ties us to Cultivate's trust-cue architecture; orange gives CTAs pop without going gradient-y.

**My call: warm teal (~#0A9294) for interactive UI + burnt orange (~#C55A2C) for primary CTAs.** Not bright cyan (Zaka territory). Not saturated blue (SaaS territory).

**Neutral: warm dark gray, not pure black.**

Body copy in **#1F2937** (warm dark gray, not #000). Reads intentional and modern. Muted text in **#5B6472**.

**Draft v0.1.1 palette (subject to real design pass):**

| Role | Hex (draft) | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Primary (navy) | #0F2540 | Wordmark, H1, primary buttons, footer |
| Background | #FFFFFF | Primary page background |
| Alt background | #F7F8F9 | Alternating section backgrounds |
| Accent teal | #0A9294 | Links, hover states, secondary buttons |
| Accent orange | #C55A2C | Primary CTA button, highlight callouts |
| Body text | #1F2937 | Body copy |
| Muted text | #5B6472 | Metadata, captions, footer text |
| Border | #E5E7EB | Card borders, dividers |

**Why this palette specifically:** navy anchors the "premium heritage" read, white background keeps it modern and crisp (not craft/warm), teal echoes Cultivate's trust-cue-adjacent accent for interactive elements, and orange gives us a CTA that pops on a navy-and-white page without going gradient. It's five colors that hold together — restraint over abundance.

## 6. Typography direction — v0.1.1 (revised)

**Sans-serif throughout.** None of the aspirational reference brands (Orases, Cultivate, Land Rover) use editorial serifs. All three lean on modern sans with strong weight hierarchy for differentiation. Original v0.1 called for Fraunces (serif) headline + Inter (sans) body — that would fight the reference set. Killed.

**Two typefaces from the same or adjacent families** — one for display, one for body. Simpler, ships everywhere, easier to maintain.

**Display candidates:**

1. **Manrope** (Google Fonts, free) — modern geometric sans with more character than Inter. Slightly rounded terminals, feels premium without going stiff. Reads Cultivate-adjacent.
2. **DM Sans** (Google Fonts, free) — cleaner and more neutral than Manrope. Reads Orases-adjacent.
3. **Inter Display** (Google Fonts, free) — Inter's display variant with tightened metrics for headline sizes. Safest, most conservative choice.
4. **My call: Manrope for display.** Character matters. DM Sans is safer but reads more generic. Inter Display works if we want to consolidate to a single family.

**Body candidates:**

1. **Inter** (Google Fonts, free) — the modern default. Clean, functional, ships everywhere. Best-in-class for UI and body copy.
2. **DM Sans** (Google Fonts, free) — if we want display and body from the same family.
3. **My call: Inter for body.** Nobody dislikes Inter. Ships everywhere. Reads crisp and modern.

**Alternate premium path (only if we have paid clients and $500 to spend):**

- **Aeonik** (paid, ~$500) — highly distinctive premium sans, used by Cash App and many premium tech brands. Would give us a real differentiator. Not for v1.
- **Söhne** (paid, ~$300) — premium sans used by Substack, OpenAI. Editorial-adjacent. Reads intentional. Post-revenue upgrade candidate.

**Type hierarchy (v0.1.1):**

- H1 (hero headline): Manrope, 48-56px, weight 700, tight leading (1.1), letter-spacing -0.02em
- H2 (section headline): Manrope, 32-36px, weight 700, leading 1.2
- H3 (subsection): Manrope, 22px, weight 600
- Body large (subheads): Inter, 20px, weight 400, leading 1.5
- Body: Inter, 17px, weight 400, leading 1.6
- Small / metadata: Inter, 14px, weight 500, letter-spacing 0.02em, muted color

**Weight hierarchy is doing the work here.** The reference brands don't rely on typeface contrast (serif vs sans) — they rely on weight contrast (700 headline against 400 body) and size hierarchy. Follow that pattern.

## 7. Iconography, imagery, illustration — v0.1.1 (revised: photos of the founders now load-bearing)

**Photography of the two founders is load-bearing, not optional.** This is the single biggest lesson from Cultivate Advisors' brand — their entire "Partner with an expert" positioning is carried by documentary-style photos of real advisors. At our stage, with no client testimonials and no case studies, **our faces ARE the trust cue.** A homepage with two clear photos of Robert and Grant is worth more than any amount of positioning copy.

Practical implication: **before the Thursday website work, one of us takes decent headshots.** Not stock, not phone-selfies. A real camera, natural light, plain background, professional but not stiff (Cultivate-style, not corporate-portrait-style). If neither of us can do it well, a 30-minute session with a local photographer is worth $150–$300 and unblocks the entire homepage. This is a Week 2 prerequisite, not a Week 4 nice-to-have.

**Additional photography options (secondary to the founder photos):**

- **Specific tool screenshots** — a real Make.com dashboard from a real client engagement (with permission and any sensitive data redacted), or a real Claude Code session. Beats abstract diagrams for showing what we actually do.
- **Documentary-style photos of client workspaces** — Cultivate does this. Not "hero shot of a happy client" but "wide shot of a founder at their kitchen table with a laptop." Later, not v1.

**Do NOT use stock photography under any circumstances.** It universally signals "we couldn't be bothered to make this real." Yoh's homepage is the case study — anonymous stock people, indistinguishable from a hundred other staffing firms.

**Icons:** functional line icons. [Lucide](https://lucide.dev) or [Phosphor](https://phosphoricons.com) at their outlined weight, colored in the teal accent (#0A9294). NOT filled shapes, NOT emoji-style, NOT AI-tech-glowing icons.

**Illustration:** none for v0.1. If we add illustration later, spot illustration only (small, purposeful) — not decorative hero art. Anti-example: don't fill the empty space above the fold with a decorative vector scene of "people collaborating." Let the founder photo + strong typography carry it.

**Charts and diagrams:** permitted and welcome. A well-drawn "here's how a discovery audit flows" diagram builds credibility. Use Inter, brand colors, thin strokes. Anti-example: don't do the Zaka-style "pyramid of tiers with glowing gradient nodes."

**Logo carousel of client industries (not clients themselves yet):** Orases uses NFL/MLB client logos. We don't have client logos yet, but we can use industry indicator icons (veterinary, insurance, DTC brand) as a "who we serve" strip. Delete once we have real client logos to swap in.

## 8. Wordmark direction — v0.1.1 (revised: sans wordmark, not editorial serif)

**Tack has three metaphorical readings** that could inform the mark:

1. **Sailing** — "to tack" means to change course into the wind. Strategic pivot. This reading fits our positioning ("we help you change course before you burn out") but risks being too metaphor-heavy for a vet who has never sailed.
2. **Horse tack** — the equipment that lets a rider control a horse. Utility and craft. Fits a rural/service-industry aesthetic but risks looking Western/agricultural.
3. **Tacking on / addressing directly** — as in "let's tack this problem." Direct, action-oriented. Fits our personality (direct, precise).

**My call: lean on reading 3, direct and action-oriented, no literal metaphor in the mark.** Confident modern sans wordmark, all lowercase or all caps. Optionally paired with a very simple geometric mark that works as a favicon and social avatar.

**Reference brand alignment for wordmark treatment:**

- **Land Rover** wordmark: all caps, geometric sans, tightly kerned, on an oval mark. Simple, confident, unmistakable at any size.
- **Orases** wordmark: modern sans in navy, slightly custom "a" — no accompanying mark, just the wordmark.
- **Cultivate** wordmark: soft sans with a simple leaf mark. Warm but professional.

Our target is closest to **Land Rover's confidence + Orases's restraint** — a wordmark that reads authoritative without needing an accompanying mark, but with an optional simple geometric companion for favicon and social.

**Wordmark v0.1.1 sketches to explore (Thursday task):**

- **"tack" (lowercase)** in Manrope 700 or a modified custom sans, tightly kerned, navy on white. Reads modern-premium.
- **"TACK" (all caps)** in a heavier weight (800–900), tighter letter-spacing, wide letterforms. Reads Land Rover confident.
- **"Tack." (with period)** — reinforces the "direct action" reading. The period is doing the personality work.
- **Optional mark:** simple geometric — a filled square dot, a small right angle, a small tick shape. Works as favicon. Not required.

**What we should NOT do:**

- A sailboat icon. Too literal. Kills the metaphor and locks us out of insurance/vet-clinic segments.
- A horse or saddle icon. Same reason, plus wrong audience signal.
- A "T" monogram in a circle. Startup-coded. Boring.
- **A serif wordmark.** Original v0.1 called for Fraunces; killing that direction — reference brands are all sans.
- **A gradient.** Signals 2023 AI startup.
- **A mark that requires the wordmark to make sense.** The wordmark must stand alone. The mark is optional companion.

## 9. Website tone in one page (Thursday reference)

The homepage should have exactly these elements in this order:

1. **One-line headline** in the voice above: what we do, in specific and human terms
2. **One sub-headline** with the specific outcome (hours back, per week)
3. **Three-bullet "how it works"** — Audit, Implement, Manage. Each with one sentence.
4. **Pricing snapshot** — three tiers with anchors. Numbers on the page. No "contact us for pricing."
5. **Who we're for** — vet clinics, insurance agencies, DTC brands. Named specifically.
6. **How we're different** — three bullets on what makes us not-Deloitte, not-a-software-vendor, not-a-fractional-COO.
7. **CTA** — book a 30-minute discovery call. That's the only conversion on the page.
8. **Founder photos + names** — real people. This is a trust play at our stage. Two founders, real names, real faces.

That's it. No testimonials (we don't have any yet). No press logos. No FAQ. No blog. No newsletter signup. The site is a business card, not a marketing funnel.
