## If you only read one section

We are the person a founder calls the day they realize the front-desk chaos is unsustainable. Not a software company. Not a big consultancy. Not an agency. A small, senior team that reads your business, builds specific automation on tools you already own, and hands you back the hours you lost to admin. We look and sound like the pros the pros use — professional enough to be trusted with a $12,000 engagement, human enough that a solo vet doesn't feel like she called Deloitte.

## 1. Positioning statement

**For** founder-run small businesses (veterinary clinics, insurance agencies, DTC brands doing $500K–$5M) **who** are losing 15+ hours a week to operational admin that scaled with the business but can't be delegated to existing staff, **Tack** provides **AI-augmented operations consulting** that **audits their workflow, implements specific automations on tools they already own (Make/Zapier/n8n), and hands the hours back.** Unlike **enterprise consultancies** (too expensive, too slow, too abstract), **software vendors** (sell a tool, leave the founder to figure it out), or **fractional COOs** (expensive full-time hire, not scoped to a project), we **deliver a fixed-fee 5-week Implementation with a defined scope, a written liability cap, and a working system at the end.**

## 2. Personality — 5 adjectives, 5 anti-adjectives

**We are:**

1. **Grounded** — we describe what actually happens ("we'll audit for a week, then build for four") rather than what sounds impressive ("transformation," "synergy," "AI-first"). No fog.
2. **Precise** — pricing is fixed. Scope is written. Deliverables are named. If we say Thursday, we mean Thursday.
3. **Warm** — we like our clients. We take a call at 4:47 PM without an eyeroll. We remember what the vet's dog is named. This is not a personality trait for marketing — it's the actual difference between us and BigCo.
4. **Modern** — we use current tools well. We don't hide the fact that Claude does substantive analysis. We don't hide that a human reviews everything. Both are the deal.
5. **Direct** — we say what we think. If a client's asking for the wrong thing, we tell them and offer an alternative. If we can't help, we tell them who can.

**We are NOT:**

1. **Not startup-coded.** No cutesy mascots, no "we're just a scrappy team of dreamers," no gradient rainbow logos, no exclamation points in headlines. Our buyers own real businesses and want real vendors.
2. **Not enterprise-coded.** No stock photos of diverse people pointing at whiteboards, no "leveraging synergies to unlock digital transformation," no six-page proposals, no logos of Fortune 500s we've never worked with.
3. **Not AI-boosterism-coded.** No robot iconography, no "the future of work is here" copy, no dashboards full of gauges nobody reads. The AI is a tool, not the story.
4. **Not agency-coded.** No portfolio pages showing 47 recent projects, no dark-mode-forever aesthetic, no "we're a full-service creative studio." We're a consultancy, not a design shop.
5. **Not chatty.** No blog full of listicles, no "5 automation trends for 2026," no newsletter with a wave emoji greeting. We publish rarely and mean it when we do.

## 3. Voice — how we sound in writing

**Three principles:**

**a. Say the thing.** No warm-up sentences. Every paragraph earns its position. "Your front desk is drowning" is a better opener than "In today's fast-paced world of veterinary care...".

**b. Use specifics.** "You're spending 12 hours a week on insurance authorizations" beats "operational overhead is a challenge." Specifics build credibility; abstractions burn it.

**c. Own the trade-off.** Every recommendation has a downside. Say it out loud. "This will save you 8 hours a week, but the first two weeks will feel harder while your team adjusts to the new workflow" is a more trustworthy sentence than the same claim without the caveat.

**Voice tests (samples we should be able to write in this voice tomorrow):**

- Homepage headline: "Get your Wednesday afternoons back." — not "AI-Powered Operations Consulting for the Modern SMB."
- Pricing page one-liner: "Fixed fees, written scope, working system at handoff." — not "Transparent pricing designed to align with your business goals."
- Discovery-call CTA: "Book a 30-minute call. We'll ask what's eating your week." — not "Schedule a free consultation to explore how our solutions can transform your business."
- Bad-news email opener: "The vendor lock-in on your PMS is worse than we hoped." — not "We've encountered some challenges with the integration we wanted to explore with you."

## 4. Reference brands — who we admire, who we don't want to look like

**Aspirational reference set (the vibe we're targeting):**

1. **[Gusto](https://gusto.com)** — trustworthy-professional. Payroll for small business. Their website reads confident and human without being casual. Green primary, warm typography, no unnecessary imagery. Solo vets and insurance agents already trust brands that look like this.
2. **[Ramp](https://ramp.com)** — precision and restraint. Fintech-grade seriousness with modern typography. Notice: they don't oversell. Their homepage tells you what the product does in one line.
3. **[Jobber](https://getjobber.com)** — approachable-utility. Home services SaaS. They know their buyer is a plumber, not a founder in a hoodie. Warm blues, real photos, plain language. Their brand says "we built this for you."
4. **[Every](https://every.to)** — publication-brand seriousness. Custom typography, generous whitespace, editorial feel. This is closer to a "senior partner" vibe than a "startup" vibe.

**Anti-reference set (things we should look and sound nothing like):**

1. **Any generic "AI consulting" firm from 2023–2025** — gradient purple-to-blue, "AI-powered" everything, hero images of hands touching glowing digital nodes. Cringe by 2026.
2. **BigCo consulting websites** (Accenture, Deloitte, EY) — sea of stock photos, jargon-heavy headlines, PDF thought leadership. Signals "not for you" to a solo vet.
3. **YC-startup homepages circa 2020** — big centered headline, "Try it free," astronaut illustration. Wrong audience signal.
4. **Fiverr/Upwork gig-economy aesthetic** — signals "cheap" when we want to signal "worth it."

## 5. Color direction — v0.1 palette proposal

**Primary color: deep navy or forest green.**

- Navy: trust, permanence, financial-services adjacent. Buyers who insure things trust navy.
- Forest green: growth, care, human. Buyers in veterinary care respond well to green.
- **My call: forest green.** Vet is our beachhead, insurance is expansion. Green plays well with both. Navy is safer but reads more like a bank than a consultancy.

**Secondary color: warm cream / off-white background.**

Not pure white. Pure white on modern displays reads sterile. A slight cream (~#FAF7F0) reads as premium and human. This is a small choice that makes a big difference on the homepage.

**Accent color: burnt orange or clay.**

Used sparingly — button hovers, active links, callout highlights. A warm accent against forest green + cream reads as intentional and human. Blue-accent would read as SaaS. Yellow would read as cheap. Orange lands.

**Neutral: warm charcoal, not black.**

Body copy in a warm charcoal (~#2C2A28) instead of #000. Softer, more editorial. Reads intentional.

**Draft v0.1 palette (subject to real design pass):**

| Role | Hex (draft) | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | #2F4A3A (forest) | Wordmark, headlines, primary CTA |
| Background | #FAF7F0 (cream) | Page background |
| Accent | #C55A2C (burnt orange) | Link hover, highlights |
| Body text | #2C2A28 (warm charcoal) | Body copy, secondary text |
| Muted | #6B665F (warm gray) | Metadata, captions |
| Border | #E5E0D6 (cream border) | Card borders, dividers |

## 6. Typography direction

**Two typefaces — one serif, one sans.** Serif for headlines and pull quotes (editorial, thoughtful). Sans for body and UI (clean, modern).

**Headline serif candidates (v0.1 recommendation):**

1. **Fraunces** (Google Fonts, free) — modern serif with a bit of character. Editorial without being pretentious. Feels like Every.to or a modern publication.
2. **Playfair Display** (Google Fonts, free) — safer, more traditional. Feels like a well-designed law firm site. Higher trust, lower personality.
3. **My call: Fraunces.** Character matters at our stage. Playfair is safer but forgettable.

**Body sans candidates:**

1. **Inter** (Google Fonts, free) — the modern default. Clean, functional, ships everywhere. Slightly generic but nobody dislikes it.
2. **Söhne** (paid, ~$300) — premium sans used by Substack, OpenAI, and many editorial brands. Reads intentional.
3. **My call: Inter for v1, upgrade to Söhne once we have paid clients.** Not worth $300 tonight.

**Type hierarchy (v0.1):**

- H1: Fraunces, 40-48px, weight 400, tight leading
- H2: Fraunces, 32px, weight 500
- H3: Inter, 20px, weight 600
- Body: Inter, 17px, weight 400, 1.6 leading
- Metadata: Inter, 14px, weight 500, warm gray

## 7. Iconography, imagery, illustration

**Icons:** functional line icons. [Lucide](https://lucide.dev) or [Phosphor](https://phosphoricons.com) at their outlined weight. NOT filled shapes, NOT emoji-style, NOT branded custom icons (yet).

**Photography:** minimal. If we use photos, they must be:
- Of real people we've actually worked with (with permission), OR
- Of specific tools/screenshots (a real Make.com dashboard, not a mockup), OR
- Not at all.

Do NOT use stock photography under any circumstances. It universally signals "we couldn't be bothered to make this real." A blank card with strong typography beats a stock photo of a smiling vet every time.

**Illustration:** none for v0.1. If we add illustration later, it should be spot illustration (small, purposeful) not decorative hero art. Think Every.to's chapter illustrations, not startup landing-page hero scenes.

**Charts and diagrams:** are permitted and welcome. A well-drawn "here's how a discovery audit flows" diagram builds credibility. Use system fonts, brand colors, thin strokes.

## 8. Wordmark direction (Tack-specific — throwaway if name changes)

**Tack has three metaphorical readings** that could inform the mark:

1. **Sailing** — "to tack" means to change course into the wind. Strategic pivot. This reading fits our positioning ("we help you change course before you burn out") but risks being too metaphor-heavy for a vet who has never sailed.
2. **Horse tack** — the equipment that lets a rider control a horse. Utility and craft. Fits a rural/service-industry aesthetic but risks looking Western/agricultural.
3. **Tacking on / addressing directly** — as in "let's tack this problem." Direct, action-oriented. Fits our personality (direct, precise).

**My call: lean on reading 3, direct and action-oriented, no literal metaphor in the mark.** A clean wordmark in Fraunces or a custom-drawn variation. Optionally: a very simple geometric mark (a small dot, a small angle, a small tick) that could work as a favicon and social avatar without requiring the word.

**Wordmark v0.1 sketches to explore (Thursday task, not tonight):**

- Fraunces "Tack" at 40pt, kerning tightened, dot on the "a" or period placement to add character
- All-caps "TACK" in a modified sans with a distinctive letterform (custom "a" or "k")
- "Tack" with a small brand mark to its left (a simple angle or tick shape)

**What we should NOT do:**

- A sailboat icon. Too literal. Kills the metaphor.
- A horse or saddle icon. Same reason, plus wrong audience signal.
- A "T" monogram in a circle. Startup-coded. Boring.
- A gradient. Signals 2023 AI startup.

## 9. Website tone in one page (Thursday reference)

The homepage should have exactly these elements in this order:

1. **One-line headline** in the voice above: what we do, in specific and human terms
2. **One sub-headline** with the specific outcome (hours back, per week)
3. **Three-bullet "how it works"** — Audit, Implement, Manage. Each with one sentence.
4. **Pricing snapshot** — three tiers with anchors. Numbers on the page. No "contact us for pricing."
5. **Who we're for** — vet clinics, insurance agencies, DTC brands. Named specifically.
6. **How we're different** — three bullets on what makes us not-Deloitte, not-a-software-vendor, not-a-fractional-COO.
7. **CTA** — book a 30-minute discovery call. That's the only conversion on the page.
8. **Founder photos + names** — real people. This is a trust play at our stage. Two founders, real names, real faces.

That's it. No testimonials (we don't have any yet). No press logos. No FAQ. No blog. No newsletter signup. The site is a business card, not a marketing funnel.
